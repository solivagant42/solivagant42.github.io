ΕΛΛΗΝΙΚΉ ΜΥΘΟΛΟΓΊΑ The World of Greek Myth

From the void of Chaos to the fall of Troy — a complete visual guide to the gods, cosmology, and story cycles that shaped Western civilisation for three thousand years.

Written by Jeevan Kumar
Reading time ~25 minutes
Diagrams 8 schematics
Covers Creation → Troy

Greek mythology is not a single book, a unified religion, or a consistent system. It is a vast, living, centuries-long accumulation of stories — told by poets, painted on pottery, performed in theatres, carved into temple friezes — that served simultaneously as theology, history, science, and moral philosophy. The Greeks used myth to explain everything: why the seasons change, why we suffer, why we die, why the powerful abuse the weak, and occasionally, why the weak outwit the powerful.

What makes Greek mythology inexhaustible is its psychological depth. The gods are not perfect beings who demand worship. They are amplified humans — consumed by jealousy, lust, pride, grief, and love in proportions we recognise but could not survive. Zeus is both the guarantor of justice and an incorrigible adulterer. Hera is both the protector of marriage and a vindictive schemer. This moral complexity is the tradition's greatest gift: it refuses the comfort of simple virtue.

"The Greeks said something about the human situation that is permanently, universally true. That is why their myths are still alive — not as curiosities from the ancient world, but as mirrors." — Robert Graves, The Greek Myths, 1955

How Kronos Lost Power

Kronos, warned by prophecy that his child would overthrow him, swallowed each newborn. Rhea substituted a stone wrapped in swaddling for Zeus, who grew up in Crete. Returned, Zeus forced Kronos to disgorge his siblings — Hestia, Demeter, Hera, Hades, Poseidon — and waged the Titanomachy: a ten-year war ending with the Titans imprisoned in Tartarus and Zeus ruling from Olympus.

Chapter III · Structure of RealityThe Greek Cosmos

For the Greeks, the universe was not infinite and indifferent. It was layered, purposeful, and populated at every level with divine or semi-divine presences. The cosmos had a clear vertical hierarchy — from the blazing heights of Olympus to the lightless depths of Tartarus — and this geography encoded a moral philosophy: nearness to the gods meant nearness to light, order, and life.

⚡ Olympus
The celestial palace of the twelve Olympians. Not a physical mountain but a realm above all weather, bathed in eternal light. Zeus's throne and court of divine governance.
☀ Aether / Sky
The upper atmosphere, pure and luminous. Domain of Helios (Sun) and Selene (Moon), traversing the sky each day and night in their divine chariots.
⊕ Earth — Gaia
The mortal world. A flat disc surrounded by the river Oceanus. Cities, forests, seas, and mountains — the stage on which myth is played out. Sacred sites marked divine presence.
🌊 Oceanus
The great river encircling the world-disc. Beyond it: the lands of the dead, the Hesperides, and the edges of the known world. Domain of the Titan Oceanus and 3,000 Oceanid nymphs.
👁 Hades — The Underworld
Kingdom of the dead, ruled by Hades and Persephone. Divided into realms of punishment (Tartarus), reward (Elysium), and the grey middle ground (Asphodel Meadows). Bordered by five sacred rivers.
🔥 Tartarus
The primordial abyss. As far below Hades as earth is below sky. Prison of the Titans after the Titanomachy. Home of the most extreme punishments — Tantalus, Sisyphus, Ixion — and of Typhon, the last great monster.
Chapter IV · The Divine Pantheon

The Twelve Olympians

The ruling gods of Olympus — each sovereign over a domain of natural and human life. Their conflicts and alliances shaped every myth, every war, and every mortal fate.

⚡
Zeus
ΖΕΥΣ · Jupiter
Sky · Thunder · Justice · Kingship
King of the gods. Wielder of the thunderbolt. Upholder of cosmic order and justice — yet himself an inveterate adulterer whose affairs generated half the myths. Symbol: eagle, oak, thunderbolt.
🦚
Hera
ΗΡΑ · Juno
Marriage · Women · Childbirth
Queen of Olympus. Protector of marriage — and consequently consumed by jealousy of Zeus's liaisons. Her vengeance against Heracles, Io, and Echo make her myth's great wronged queen. Symbol: peacock, cow, pomegranate.
🔱
Poseidon
ΠΟΣΕΙΔΩΝ · Neptune
Sea · Earthquakes · Horses
Lord of the seas and of earthquakes ("Earth-Shaker"). Drew lots with Zeus and Hades for dominion. Temperamental, prone to grudges — his enmity with Odysseus drove the Odyssey. Symbol: trident, horse, dolphin.
🌾
Demeter
ΔΗΜΗΤΗΡ · Ceres
Harvest · Agriculture · Seasons
Goddess of the grain and harvest. Her grief over Persephone's abduction created winter — the first myth of seasonal change. Her Eleusinian Mysteries were the most widely practiced religious rites in the ancient world. Symbol: wheat, torch, corn.
🦉
Athena
ΑΘΗΝΑ · Minerva
Wisdom · Warfare (Strategy) · Crafts
Born fully formed and armoured from Zeus's head. Goddess of intellect, not brute force — her warfare is strategic, not passionate. Patron of Athens, with the Parthenon as her ultimate tribute. Symbol: owl, olive tree, aegis.
🏹
Apollo
ΑΠΟΛΛΩΝ · Apollo
Sun · Music · Prophecy · Medicine
The god of light, arts, and reason. His oracle at Delphi was the most authoritative voice in the ancient world. Twin of Artemis. His arrows brought plague; his medicine cured. The most "Greek" of gods — reason, beauty, measure. Symbol: lyre, laurel, sun, bow.
🌙
Artemis
ΑΡΤΕΜΙΣ · Diana
Hunt · Moon · Wilderness · Chastity
Twin of Apollo. Virgin huntress of the wilderness, protector of young women and girls. Her independence was absolute — Actaeon died for accidentally seeing her bathe. Both healer and destroyer of women in childbirth. Symbol: crescent moon, deer, bow.
⚔
Ares
ΑΡΗΣ · Mars
War (Bloodlust) · Violence · Courage
God of war's violent, chaotic aspect — distinguished from Athena's strategic warfare. Universally disliked by the other gods; even Zeus called him the most hateful. Lover of Aphrodite; father of Phobos (Fear) and Deimos (Terror). Symbol: spear, shield, vulture.
🌹
Aphrodite
ΑΦΡΟΔΙΤΗ · Venus
Love · Beauty · Desire · Procreation
Born from sea-foam where Uranus's severed genitals fell. The most dangerous of gods: her power — desire — operates on immortals and mortals alike. Her judgment of Paris sparked the Trojan War. Married to Hephaestus; lover of Ares. Symbol: dove, rose, myrtle.
🔨
Hephaestus
ΗΦΑΙΣΤΟΣ · Vulcan
Fire · Forge · Craftsmanship
Divine craftsman and smith. Lame (thrown from Olympus as an infant — either by Hera, or by Zeus in a quarrel). Created divine armour, palaces, and Pandora's box. His lameness made him the gods' outsider — perhaps why he was the most human. Symbol: hammer, anvil, fire.
☿
Hermes
ΕΡΜΗΣ · Mercury
Messengers · Travel · Commerce · Thieves
Divine messenger, guide of souls to Hades, patron of travelers, merchants, and thieves. The cleverest of gods — the only one who moved freely between all realms. Born in the morning; by evening had invented the lyre and stolen Apollo's cattle. Symbol: caduceus, winged sandals, petasus.
🍇
Dionysus
ΔΙΟΝΥΣΟΣ · Bacchus
Wine · Ecstasy · Theatre · Madness
Twice-born god — first from his mortal mother Semele (killed by Zeus's glory), then from Zeus's thigh. God of liberation: wine loosened social bonds, theatre allowed transformation, ecstasy dissolved the self. Nietzsche's Dionysian principle. Symbol: grapevine, thyrsus, leopard.

Note: Hestia (goddess of the hearth) sometimes replaces Dionysus in the canonical twelve. The list was never entirely fixed — different cities honoured different configurations.

Chapter V · Hesiod's Works and Days

The Five Ages of Man

Hesiod described a progressive decline from an original golden perfection into the troubled present — a mythology of history as degeneration. Each age is defined by its relationship to the gods, to labour, and to justice.

Golden Age
Under Kronos · First Generation
The Age of Perfection
Humans lived like gods — no toil, no sorrow, no old age. The earth gave its fruits freely. Death came gently, like sleep. These first humans became the good daimons, guardian spirits of the earth after death.
No Labour No War No Disease No Old Age Feasts with Gods
Silver Age
Under Zeus · Second Generation
The Age of Childhood
Humans spent a hundred years as children, foolish and undisciplined. Their adult lives were short and full of suffering — their own fault, as they refused to honour the gods or each other. Zeus destroyed them and buried them underground.
Impiety Childhood Extended Brief Adulthood
Bronze Age
Warriors · Third Generation
The Age of War
A race of terrible warriors — powerful, hard, and warlike as bronze itself. Interested only in violence. They destroyed themselves through their own hands. Their spirits descended nameless into Hades, leaving no legacy.
Total War No Arts Self-Destruction
Heroic Age
Demigods · Fourth Generation
The Age of Heroes
A nobler race — the demigods, born of gods and mortals. Perseus, Achilles, Heracles, Odysseus. More just and godlike than their predecessors. They fought at Thebes and Troy. The best of them were translated to the Isles of the Blest after death — paradise.
Demigods Epic Warfare Divine Favour Isles of the Blest
Iron Age
The Present · Fifth Generation
The Age of Toil
Our own age — and the worst. Full of labour, grief, strife, and injustice. The gods have withdrawn. Justice and Shame have fled the earth. Children dishonour parents; strangers abuse guests. Hesiod writes: "Would that I were not among the fifth generation of men, but either had died before or been born afterwards."
Endless Labour Injustice Gods Absent Present Day
Chapter VI · The Kingdom of Hades

The Underworld — Map of the Dead

The Greek afterlife was not heaven or hell but a complex geography — a realm of rivers, judges, and carefully differentiated fates. All the dead went there; what mattered was where you ended up.

Asphodel Meadows
ΤΑ ΑΣΦΟΔΕΛΑ ΛΕΙΜΩΝΙΑ

The grey majority. Ordinary souls who were neither exceptionally virtuous nor wicked. They wander in a dim, joyless plain, drinking from the Lethe and forgetting their mortal lives. Homer's Achilles would rather be a slave among the living than a king here.

Elysium · Isles of the Blest
ΗΛΥΣΙΟΝ · ΜΑΚΑΡΩΝ ΝΗΣΟΙ

Paradise — reserved for heroes, the exceptionally virtuous, and those chosen by the gods. Eternal sunshine, feasts, music, and games. Souls who reach Elysium three times may enter the Isles of the Blest — ultimate paradise, beyond even Elysium.

Fields of Punishment
ΕΡΕΒΟΣ

For the wicked and impious. Not simply imprisonment, but specifically tailored punishments. Tantalus reaches for fruit forever just out of reach. Sisyphus rolls his boulder uphill forever. Ixion spins on a burning wheel. Punishment that mirrors the crime.

The Palace of Hades
ΔΩΜΑΤΑ ΑΪΔΑΟ

Hades rules with Persephone, queen of the dead. Guarded by Cerberus, the three-headed hound who prevents the living from entering and the dead from leaving. The court of three judges — Minos, Rhadamanthus, Aeacus — determines each soul's fate.

🔥 Tartarus — The Abyss

As far below Hades as earth is below sky. Prison of the Titans. The worst of the wicked. Typhon, the last great monster, is here. The bronze gates are guarded by the Erinyes (Furies). No soul returns unaided — Orpheus and Heracles being the only mortals to descend and return.

Chapter VII · The Heroic Tradition

The Great Hero Cycles

Greek heroes are not simply strong — they are human beings pushed to extremes, discovering both their greatness and their fatal flaws. Each major hero cycle follows a structural pattern: divine parentage, impossible labours, and a tragic or transcendent end.

Hero Cycle I
Heracles
Greatest of Heroes · Son of Zeus & Alcmene
Birth: Zeus fathers him on Alcmene; Hera's enmity begins immediately — she sends serpents to kill the infant.
Madness: Hera drives him mad; in a frenzy he kills his own wife and children. His penance: the Twelve Labours.
The Twelve Labours: Nemean Lion · Lernaean Hydra · Cerynean Hind · Erymanthian Boar · Augean Stables · Stymphalian Birds · Cretan Bull · Mares of Diomedes · Belt of Hippolyta · Cattle of Geryon · Apples of the Hesperides · Cerberus.
Death & Apotheosis: Poisoned by the Hydra-blood shirt of Nessus — his wife, Deianeira, believes it a love charm. Burns on his pyre and ascends to Olympus, finally reconciled with Hera and married to Hebe.
"Strength without wisdom destroys the self. Suffering is not punishment — it is the price of greatness."
Hero Cycle II
Perseus
Slayer of Medusa · Son of Zeus & Danaë
Origin: King Acrisius imprisons his daughter Danaë to prevent fulfilment of a prophecy. Zeus visits as golden rain. Perseus is born.
The Quest: King Polydectes demands Medusa's head. Perseus receives divine gifts: Hermes's winged sandals, Athena's mirrored shield, Hades's helm of invisibility.
Medusa: Views her only in the shield's reflection; beheads her. From her neck spring Pegasus and Chrysaor. He carries the head — a weapon that turns to stone — in the kibisis bag.
Andromeda: Rescues the Ethiopian princess chained as sacrifice to the sea monster Cetus. Uses Medusa's head. They marry. He fulfils the prophecy by accidentally killing Acrisius at athletic games.
"The hero's tools come from the gods, but the courage to use them is his own. Fate cannot be escaped — only navigated."
Hero Cycle III
Odysseus
The Wanderer · King of Ithaca · Son of Laertes
After Troy: Ten years of wandering after the war — Poseidon's wrath because Odysseus blinded his son, the Cyclops Polyphemus.
Obstacles: Sirens · Scylla & Charybdis · Circe (who turns men to pigs) · Polyphemus · the Laestrygonians · Calypso (seven years' captivity) · the Land of the Dead (Nekyia).
The Nekyia: Descends to the underworld — unique among living heroes — to consult the shade of Tiresias. Meets Achilles, his mother, and the great heroes of the past.
Return: Arrives home in disguise. Passes the test of the bow — only he can string it. Kills the suitors. Reunited with Penelope after twenty years.
"Intelligence and endurance outlast brute strength. Home is worth every ordeal — but you become who you are through the journey."
Hero Cycle IV
Achilles
Greatest Warrior · Son of Peleus & Thetis
The Prophecy: Either a long, obscure life or a short life of eternal glory. Achilles chose glory — and the Iliad is its accounting.
The Heel: Thetis dipped him in the Styx for invulnerability, holding him by the heel. His one vulnerability — a mythological addition; Homer doesn't mention it.
The Wrath: The entire Iliad turns on his rage at Agamemnon's dishonour — his withdrawal from battle, his return after Patroclus's death, his slaying of Hector.
Death: Apollo guides Paris's arrow to his heel. Dies outside the walls of Troy — never entering the city he effectively destroys.
"The choice between safety and glory defines a life. Wrath is power — but also destruction. Even the greatest hero is mortal."
Hero Cycle V
Orpheus
The Musician · Son of Apollo & Calliope
The Gift: Music so perfect that trees followed his lyre, rivers stopped, and wild beasts became tame. Sailed with the Argonauts — drowned out the Sirens with his song.
Eurydice: His wife bitten by a serpent on their wedding day. Orpheus descends to Hades — the only mortal to do so through sheer beauty of music.
The Condition: Hades grants Eurydice's return on one condition: Orpheus must not look back as he leads her out. He cannot bear the uncertainty — at the threshold, he turns.
Death: Torn apart by Bacchantes (Maenads) whom he refused. His head, still singing, floated down the river Hebrus to the sea.
"Love's greatest enemy is doubt. Art can move death itself — but cannot survive the human need for certainty."
Hero Cycle VI
Oedipus
The Tragic King · Son of Laius & Jocasta
The Oracle: Apollo prophecies that Laius will be killed by his own son. The infant Oedipus is left to die on a mountainside, feet pierced. Adopted by the King of Corinth.
Flight: Learning the prophecy himself, Oedipus flees Corinth — towards Thebes. At a crossroads, kills an old man in a quarrel. The man is Laius. His father.
The Sphinx: Solves the riddle ("What walks on four legs in the morning, two at noon, three in the evening?"). Answer: Man. Becomes King of Thebes. Marries the widowed queen. His mother.
Discovery: The truth emerges piece by piece. Jocasta hangs herself. Oedipus blinds himself with her brooches. Exiles himself to Colonus — where he dies, mysteriously, transfigured.
"Fate cannot be escaped by fleeing it. The investigation of truth, even when it destroys us, is the most human act. Self-knowledge is both the wound and the cure."
Chapter VIII · The Epic Cycle

The Trojan War — A Timeline

The central myth-event of Greek civilisation — drawn from the Iliad, Odyssey, and the lost Epic Cycle. Ten years of war over one woman, driven by the gods, and ending in the destruction of a civilisation.

I
The Seed of War
The Wedding of Peleus & Thetis

Eris (Discord), not invited, throws a golden apple inscribed "For the Fairest." Hera, Athena, and Aphrodite each claim it. Zeus refuses to judge. The task falls to Paris, prince of Troy — a shepherd on Mount Ida.

II
The Judgment
The Judgment of Paris

Each goddess offers a bribe. Hera: political power. Athena: wisdom and martial skill. Aphrodite: the most beautiful woman in the world — Helen, wife of Menelaus of Sparta. Paris chooses Aphrodite. The other goddesses become implacable enemies of Troy.

III
Abduction · c. 1194 BCE (myth-time)
Helen Departs for Troy

Paris visits Sparta as a guest of Menelaus. Violating xenia (sacred hospitality), he abducts Helen while Menelaus is away in Crete. Whether Helen went willingly — seduced by Aphrodite's power, or in love, or as a captive — is never resolved. The ambiguity is the myth's heart.

IV
The Gathering
Agamemnon Musters the Greeks

Menelaus appeals to his brother Agamemnon, High King of Mycenae. Under the oath of Tyndareus (all suitors of Helen swore to defend whoever won her), the Greek kings are bound to war. The fleet gathers at Aulis — and stalls there, becalmed, until Agamemnon sacrifices his daughter Iphigenia to Artemis for wind.

V
The War · Years 1–9
Nine Years Before the Iliad

Homer's Iliad covers only fifty-one days in the tenth year. The preceding nine years: raids on surrounding cities, failed negotiations, the death of Protesilaus (first Greek to land — first to die), raids on Tenedos, Chryse, Lesbos. The war that seemed it would end quickly instead hardened into a siege.

VI
The Iliad · Year 10
The Wrath of Achilles

Agamemnon takes Briseis, Achilles's prize-captive. Achilles withdraws from battle. The Greeks falter catastrophically. Zeus honours his promise to Thetis — to make the Greeks suffer until they appreciate Achilles. Hector kills Patroclus (wearing Achilles's armour). Achilles returns, kills Hector, desecrates his body. Priam's midnight embassy recovers his son's corpse.

VII
After the Iliad
The Death of Achilles & the Wooden Horse

Amazon queen Penthesilea and Ethiopian king Memnon fight for Troy; both killed by Achilles. Apollo guides Paris's arrow to Achilles's heel. Paris killed by Philoctetes's bow (Heracles's divine weapon). Odysseus devises the Trojan Horse — a hollow wooden structure left as an offering, filled with Greek warriors. The Trojans bring it inside their walls. At night: the Greeks emerge. Troy burns.

VIII
Aftermath
The Nostos — The Returns

The sack of Troy violated the sacred — Cassandra raped at Athena's altar, Priam killed at Zeus's altar. The gods punished the Greeks in their homecomings (nostos). Agamemnon: murdered by Clytemnestra on arrival. Ajax: drowned by Poseidon. Menelaus: stranded in Egypt for seven years. Odysseus: ten years of wandering. Only Nestor returned swiftly and safely.

Chapter IX · Why It Still MattersThe Legacy of Myth

Greek mythology is not ancient history. It is the operating system beneath Western civilisation's surface. Every time we use the word narcissism, we invoke Narcissus. Panic comes from Pan. An odyssey is Odysseus. The planets Mars, Venus, Mercury, Jupiter, Saturn, Neptune are the Roman names for the same gods. Music comes from the Muses. Chaos is the Greek primordial void. The very word psychology comes from Psyche.

But beyond etymology, Greek myth endures because its psychological insights are accurate. It describes, with narrative precision, what it feels like to be at the mercy of forces larger than yourself — called gods because they are overwhelming, but recognisable as pride, desire, jealousy, grief. The Greeks invented the concept of hubris — not simply arrogance, but the specific human tendency to overreach, to place oneself in the territory of the gods. Every Greek tragedy is a meditation on where the limits of human agency lie.

"The Greeks did not make their gods into perfect beings. They made them into amplified humans — and in doing so, they said something true about the nature of power that political philosophers have been trying to say ever since." — Bernard Knox, The Oldest Dead White European Males, 1993

Myth as Philosophy

The pre-Socratic philosophers arose directly from the mythological tradition — and largely replaced its supernatural explanations with natural ones. Thales replaced Poseidon (water as divine power) with water as fundamental element. Heraclitus replaced Zeus (lightning-order) with Logos (reason-order). The myths weren't simply wrong and replaced by philosophy; they were philosophy in narrative form, translated into abstract argument.

Plato himself used myth — the Cave, the Ring of Gyges, the Myth of Er — precisely because he understood that narrative reaches parts of the mind that argument cannot. The border between Greek myth and Greek philosophy is permeable. Both are attempts to answer the same questions: Why do we suffer? What is justice? What survives death? What does it mean to be human?

The Living Tradition

From Virgil to Shakespeare to Keats to Freud — who named the Oedipus Complex and the Narcissistic personality after Greek myths — to Joyce's Ulysses to contemporary novels by Madeline Miller and Pat Barker, Greek mythology has never stopped generating new thought. Each generation discovers in these stories a mirror: not of the ancient world, but of itself.

The myths survive not because the Greeks were exceptional but because human nature has not changed. We still make the choices that drive tragedy. We still face the questions the myths address. We still, occasionally, catch a glimpse of something larger than ourselves in the behaviour of lightning, the vastness of the sea, the silence of a forest at dusk — and understand why those ancient minds populated those forces with personalities. It is not primitive. It is honest.

An Observation

The Greeks built their myths not to comfort but to clarify. A mythology that includes the Erinyes — the Furies who pursue blood guilt — and the Moirai who cut the thread of life regardless of virtue, is not a mythology of easy consolation. It is a mythology of clear-eyed realism: the world is not just, the powerful abuse the weak, suffering is not deserved, and yet — life is worth the ordeal. That combination of unflinching realism and affirmation is the tradition's greatest achievement, and its permanent value.
