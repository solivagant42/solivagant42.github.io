Mythos
Mythology

Gods, Cosmos,
Death & Eternity
— The Ancient Mind

The great mythological traditions are not primitive superstition. They are humanity’s earliest attempts to answer the hardest questions — what is the cosmos, how should we live, what happens when we die — through the medium of story and symbol. Across Greece and Egypt, the questions were the same; the answers were as different as the landscapes that produced them.

COSMOGONY

Creation from Chaos

Before gods and earth — only Chaos. From the void emerged Gaia, Tartarus, Eros, and Nyx. The Greeks imagined not creation ex nihilo but emergence: order arising from undifferentiated void.

THEOGONY

The Divine Genealogy

Titans born of Gaia and Uranus. Kronos devours his children until Zeus outmanoeuvres him. The Olympians seize power after ten years of war — the Titanomachy. Power passes through cunning, not strength alone.

PANTHEON

The Twelve Olympians

Zeus, Hera, Poseidon, Demeter, Athena, Apollo, Artemis, Ares, Aphrodite, Hephaestus, Hermes, Dionysus — each sovereign of a human domain, each embodying a force that cannot be suppressed.

HESIOD · c.700 BCE

The Five Ages of Man

Gold, Silver, Bronze, Heroic, Iron — a myth of progressive decline. We live in the Iron Age: full of toil, injustice, and absent gods. Hesiod wished he had been born in any other age.

HERO TRADITION

Perseus, Heracles, Odysseus

The Greek hero cycle: divine parentage, impossible labours, a fatal flaw, a transcendent or tragic end. Heroes are the bridge between gods and mortals — more than human, but not exempt from suffering.

THE EPIC CYCLE

The Trojan War

A golden apple, a beauty judgment, an abduction, ten years of siege, a wooden horse. The war that generated Homer's Iliad and Odyssey — the twin foundations of Western literature.

Cosmic Structure
The Greek Vertical Universe

From Olympus at the peak to Tartarus at the abyss — the Greek cosmos was a moral geography. Proximity to the gods meant proximity to light and order.

Olympus↓ Aether↓ Earth · Gaia↓ Hades↓ Tartarus
The Underworld
Five Rivers of the Dead

Styx (Hatred) · Acheron (Woe) · Lethe (Oblivion) · Phlegethon (Fire) · Cocytus (Lamentation) — the geography of death as moral landscape.

Styx→ Charon's Ferry→ Judgment→ Elysium / Tartarus
II · Egyptian Mythology

Egyptian mythology is the oldest continuously practised religious tradition in recorded human history — nearly three and a half thousand years of theology organised around a single question: how do we maintain order against the eternal pressure of chaos? From the primordial waters of Nun to the weighing of the heart before Osiris, it remains one of the most complete and philosophically sophisticated mythological traditions the world has produced.

COSMOGONY

Nun & the Primordial Waters

Before creation there was only Nun — infinite, dark, formless potential. Egyptian creation is not ex nihilo but emergence: the world arising from the waters like the first land appearing after the Nile’s flood.

SOLAR THEOLOGY

Ra’s Daily Journey

Every sunrise is a creation. Every sunset is a death. Ra traverses the sky in his solar barque, descends into the twelve hours of the Duat at night, and is reborn as Khepri each dawn — the template for all resurrection.

THE CENTRAL MYTH

Osiris, Isis & Resurrection

The murdered king reassembled by his wife, the first mummification, the child raised to avenge his father — the myth that gave every Egyptian hope of resurrection and structured the theology of the afterlife.

THE AFTERLIFE

The Judgment of the Dead

In the Hall of Two Truths, the heart is weighed against the feather of Ma’at. If it is lighter, eternal life. If heavier, Ammit the Devourer consumes it, and the soul is annihilated — the second death.

MA’AT

Cosmic Order vs. Isfet

Ma’at — simultaneously goddess, moral code, and cosmic principle — is the foundation of all Egyptian civilisation. The opposite is Isfet: chaos pressing in from the primordial dark. All mythology is Ma’at’s defence.

DIVINE KINGSHIP

The Pharaoh as Horus

The living pharaoh was Horus — the divine falcon, the earthly form of cosmic order. In death he became Osiris. The new king became the new Horus. This cycle, maintained for three thousand years, was never broken.

The Solar Cycle
Ra’s Three Aspects

The sun god had three forms across the day — each a different theological statement about the nature of creation, power, and age.

Khepri · Dawn→ Ra-Horakhty · Noon→ Atum · Dusk
The Afterlife
The Path to the Field of Reeds

The Egyptian journey after death was not automatic — it required navigation, declaration, and judgment before the eternal life could begin.

Death→ Duat→ Judgment→ Aaru

The Eleusinian Mysteries

The most widely practised religious rites of antiquity — two thousand years of ceremony, then silence. What we know, what we don’t.

Gilgamesh, the Great Flood, the descent of Inanna — the oldest written mythology in the world and its permanent influence on all that followed.

Music
Music

Sound as the
Architecture
of Feeling

Music is the only art that exists entirely in time — you cannot revisit a moment the way you can look again at a painting. That makes it the most mysterious and the most human of all the arts.

BAROQUE · 1600–1750

Johann Sebastian Bach

The architecture of counterpoint — voices in mathematically perfect conversation. The Well-Tempered Clavier is the Bible of Western music: both a technical treatise and an act of profound beauty.

CLASSICAL · 1750–1820

Mozart & Haydn

The mastery of form — the sonata, the symphony, the string quartet. Mozart's late symphonies achieve a perfection of balance that has never been surpassed. Complexity made to feel effortless.

ROMANTIC · 1820–1900

Beethoven Onward

When Beethoven lost his hearing and kept composing, something new entered music: the idea that art could be a direct expression of the artist's inner struggle. The self became the subject.

20th CENTURY

Jazz & Ravel

Two parallel revolutions: Jazz brought rhythmic liberation and improvisational freedom from America's African heritage. Ravel brought harmonic colour of an almost painterly richness from France.

MINIMALISM

Arvo Pärt & Glass

After the maximalism of the 20th century, a return to stillness. Pärt's tintinnabuli method — music stripped to its barest harmonic bones — achieves a silence within sound.

"Without music, life would be a mistake."— Friedrich Nietzsche

Nature
Natural History

The World
As It Actually
Is

Natural history is the oldest human obsession — the desire to name, classify, and understand the living world. It requires the same careful attention as art, the same patience as music, and the same humility before something greater than yourself.

Deep Time

The Earth is 4.5 billion years old. Life has existed for 3.8 billion years. Homo sapiens have been here for 300,000 years — a single blink. Understanding geological time is perhaps the most radical shift in perspective available to any human mind. It doesn't diminish us; it contextualizes us.

The fossil record is the most extraordinary library ever discovered — a history of every creature that ever lived, written in stone. Every ammonite spiral is a chapter. Every trilobite is a sentence.

Tempus edax rerum — Time, the devourer of all things

Entomology

There are more species of beetle than of any other animal. The diversity of insect life is so vast as to be almost incomprehensible — and yet each species occupies a precise ecological niche.

Coleoptera · 400,000 species

Botany & Plant Intelligence

Plants communicate through underground fungal networks. Some trees live for thousands of years. The oldest known individual organism is a bristlecone pine — 5,000 years old.

Arbor vitae — Tree of life

The Ocean

95% of Earth's living space is ocean. We have better maps of the surface of Mars than of the ocean floor. The deep sea contains creatures that seem more alien than anything we have imagined.

Mare incognitum

Evolutionary Biology

Darwin's insight — descent with modification through natural selection — is the single most powerful idea in the history of biology. It connects every living thing to every other.

Omnis cellula e cellula
Light
Photography

Light Written
on Silver & Time

Photography means "writing with light" — from the Greek phōs (light) and graphé (writing). Every photograph is a sentence written by photons. The photographer chooses only when to let the light in, and for how long.

What interests me most is the paradox at photography's heart: it appears to capture objective reality — a moment as it actually was — but every photograph is a radical subjective act. What is included, what is excluded, where the light falls, what moment is chosen — each decision reflects a mind as much as a scene.

01

Light & Shadow

Chiaroscuro exists in photography as much as in Caravaggio. The relationship between what is illuminated and what is swallowed by darkness is the fundamental grammar of the image.

02

The Decisive Moment

Henri Cartier-Bresson called it l'instant décisif — the fraction of a second when form, content, and meaning align perfectly. Most photographers spend their lives hunting it.

03

Time Made Visible

Every photograph is also a document of entropy. The people in old photographs are all dead now. That knowledge transforms every image into something vertiginous — a moment stolen from time.
